%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\chapter{Study Area, Data, and Methodology}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter introduces the geographic scope, data sources, and mathematical models used to evaluate flood physical risk. The modeling framework is structured around three key components: \textbf{hazard} (depth), \textbf{vulnerability} (depth-damage functions), and \textbf{exposure} (asset value). These components are formally defined and then combined to estimate flood-related losses using both scenario-based calculations (e.g., damage under a 100-year flood) and probabilistic methods (e.g., Expected Annual Damage (EAD) integrated across multiple return periods (RP)).


%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Hazard and Depth}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Hazard is defined as the probability and intensity of a climate-related event. Flood hazard refers to the intensity and spatial extent of flooding, typically expressed as inundation depth for different return periods. In this study, hazard is represented as a function that maps location and return period to predicted water depth.

\subsection{Mathematical Definition of Flood Hazard}

Flood hazard is modeled as a spatially varying flood depth field:

\begin{equation}
	h : \Omega \times \mathcal{RP} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}, \quad (x, y, RP) \mapsto h(x, y, RP)
	\label{eq:hazard-map}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \( \Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2 \) is the spatial domain (study area),
	\item \( \mathcal{RP} = \{ RP_1, RP_2, \dots, RP_K \} \) is the set of modeled return periods (e.g., 10, 100, 500 years),
	\item \( h(x, y, RP) \) is the flood depth at location \( (x, y) \) under return period \( RP \).
\end{itemize}

The return period \( RP \) expresses the average time between flood events of that magnitude, while the annual exceedance probability \(AEP \) is its inverse. For example, RP100 corresponds to a 100-year return period, which is equivalent to an \(AEP \) of 1/100 - or 1\% chance of occurrence per year.

\begin{equation}
	AEP = \frac{1}{RP}
	\label{eq:aep-definition}
\end{equation}

Flood hazard maps \( h(x, y, RP) \) are derived from hydrological and hydraulic models that simulate flood dynamics based on rainfall, runoff, topography, and river systems. In practice, the continuous function \( h(x, y, RP) \) is approximated by raster arrays \( H_{i,j,k} \), where \( i,j \) index the spatial grid and \( k \) indexes the return period.

\subsection{Hazard Data Sources and Modeling Approach} \label{sec:hazard-data-sources-and-modeling-approach}

This study uses two complementary hazard models for flood depth estimation: the global \textit{WRI Aqueduct River Flood Model} and the local \textit{Integralna karta globin (IKG)}. Both models are described in more detail in the following sections and a short comparison between the two models is presented in Table \ref{tab:hazard_models}.

\subsubsection*{WRI Aqueduct River Flood Model}
The WRI Aqueduct River Flood Model is a public-domain global flood hazard dataset developed by the World Resources Institute. It provides flood depth estimates at ~1 km resolution for return periods from 2 to 1000 years, using simulations based on historical river discharge, terrain data (e.g., MERIT DEM), and land surface parameters. The dataset is based on the modelling approach of \cite{ward_assessing_2013}. Critically, this model incorporates climate scenarios through Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs), including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RCP4.5}: A moderate greenhouse gas emissions pathway that assumes the implementation of climate policies and stabilization of radiative forcing at 4.5 W/m\textsuperscript{2} by the year 2100. This scenario reflects a future in which emissions peak around 2040 and then decline due to technological improvements, energy efficiency measures, and a shift towards lower-carbon energy sources. It leads to an approximate global average temperature increase of 1.8--2.6\textdegree C by 2100, depending on model sensitivity.
    
    \item \textbf{RCP8.5}: A high-emissions scenario often referred to as a "business-as-usual" trajectory, assuming no significant climate mitigation policies are implemented. Radiative forcing reaches 8.5 W/m\textsuperscript{2} by 2100, primarily due to continued reliance on fossil fuels, high population growth, and limited technological progress in energy efficiency. It results in a projected global average temperature increase exceeding 4\textdegree C by 2100, with more severe regional impacts and increased frequency and intensity of extreme events such as floods, droughts, and heatwaves.
\end{itemize}
Outputs are provided in GeoTIFF raster format (EPSG:4326).

\subsubsection*{Integralna karta globin (IKG)}
IKG is a high-resolution local flood hazard dataset produced as part of Slovenia’s implementation of the EU Floods Directive (2007/60/EC). The models are commissioned by Slovenia’s Ministry of Natural Resources and Spatial Planning via the Slovenian Water Agency. Hydraulic modeling and cartographic production are carried out by many different engineering firms (e.g., iS Projekt d.o.o., DRI d.o.o., Inštitut za vode Republike Slovenije, and others), typically for designated \textit{Areas of Significant Flood Risk} (\textit{Območja pomembnega vpliva poplav} or OPVP).

IKG models are based on 1D or 2D hydraulic simulations using high-resolution LiDAR-derived terrain data. Flood simulations are performed under synthetic scenarios corresponding to return periods of 10, 100, and 500 years, and results are expressed in three standardized flood depth classes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Gm - Minor flooding (\(<0.5\) m),
    \item Gs - Medium flooding (\(0.5-1.5\) m),
    \item Gv - Severe flooding (\(>1.5\) m).
\end{itemize}
The outputs are published as vector layers (shapefiles or GeoPackages) and made available through the national GIS portal \cite{si_ikg_data_2025}. These layers use the national Slovenian coordinate system (EPSG:3794). Unlike global models, IKG datasets do not incorporate future climate change scenarios, and their coverage is limited to selected areas of importance identified as flood-prone under national flood risk management planning.

Depth classes for all 86 areas of importance are officially published only for RP100 \cite{si_ikg_flood_maps_methodology_2020}. This coverage is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ikg-coverage-rp100}. Depth data is also available for RP10 and RP500, but only for 18 areas of importance and is only available on request throught the Ministry of Natural Resources and Spatial Planning (for the purpose of this thesis, the data for RP10 and RP500 was requested and recieved through email). Because of this technical limitation, this thesis focuses on analyzing IKG model performance only for RP100. However, this thesis also establishes a data processing and onboarding pipeline for all return periods. This pipeline is described in Chapter~\ref{chap:model-integration-and-analysis-framework}. This will allow for straightforward analysis of other return periods when the data is available.

\section{Vulnerability and Damage Function}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Vulnerability is defined as the expected damage to an asset given the intensity of the hazard. Specifically, flood vulnerability quantifies the degree to which an asset is susceptible to damage when exposed to flooding. In quantitative modeling, this is typically represented using \textbf{depth--damage functions} (DDFs), which estimate the fraction of asset value lost as a function of water depth.

\subsection{Mathematical Definition of Flood Vulnerability}

A depth--damage function \( D(h) \) maps flood depth \( h \) (in meters) to the expected relative damage \( D(h) \in [0, 1] \):

\begin{equation}
	D : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \rightarrow [0, 1], \quad h \mapsto D(h)
	\label{eq:damage-function}
\end{equation}

These functions are typically monotonically increasing and piecewise continuous. By construction, \(D(h)\) is defined from \(h=0\) upward (including very shallow depths), and it is capped at \(1\), which corresponds to a total loss equal to the estimated \emph{property value} \(E\). This is discussed in more detail in Section~\ref{sec:exposure-and-property-value}.

\subsection{Vulnerability Data Source}

This thesis uses standardized global depth--damage functions published by Huizinga et al.~\cite{huizinga_global_2017}, developed under the European Commission Joint Research Centre (JRC). These functions are provided for different property types (residential, commercial, industrial, and agricultural buildings), and different regions (Europe, North America, Africa, and Asia). They are expressed as relative damage fractions (share of the property value lost at a given water depth).

Figure~\ref{fig:ddf-jrc-europe} shows the European depth--damage function for different property types, based on Huizinga et al.~\cite{huizinga_global_2017}. The visualization shows that the same flood depth can result in different damage fractions, depending on the property type.

\section{Exposure and Property Value}
\label{sec:exposure-and-property-value}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Exposure is the presence and value of assets located in potentially affected areas. In the scope of this thesis, exposure refers to the monetary value of physical assets (property) potentially affected by flooding.

While this thesis focuses on property value as the main measure of exposure, flood risk assessments in research and policy often use a broader range of exposure metrics, depending on the application context:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Property value}: absolute (EUR) or relative (share of building replacement value), representing the monetary worth of physical assets. This is the primary metric used in this thesis.
    \item \textbf{Population exposure}: number of people living in flood-prone areas, often used in disaster risk reduction and emergency planning.
    \item \textbf{Infrastructure exposure}: critical facilities (e.g., hospitals, schools, energy or transport networks) located in inundation zones.
    \item \textbf{Land use / area exposed}: total inundated surface, disaggregated by categories such as agricultural, industrial, or residential land.
    \item \textbf{Economic activity exposure}: gross domestic product (GDP) or sectoral output situated in floodplains, relevant for macroeconomic stress tests.
    \item \textbf{Cultural and ecological assets}: heritage sites, protected areas, or ecosystems whose value is not fully captured in monetary terms.
\end{itemize}

These alternatives highlight the flexibility of exposure modeling. However, for the purposes of this thesis, using property value ensures consistency with the standardized depth--damage functions and allows for direct validation of model predictions against the observed flood damage dataset.


Importantly, the first part of the validation analysis was conducted in \textbf{relative terms} — comparing observed and predicted \textit{fractional damage} (i.e., damage as a share of estimated property value), rather than in absolute monetary losses. This avoids the need for detailed cadastral registry data and reduces dependence on asset-specific exposure assumptions. This was done to focus the analysis on hazard and vulnerability model performance, rather than on variability in asset valuation.

While this approach abstracts away exposure uncertainty, it enables transparent model comparison. Integration of more granular exposure data (e.g. official appraisal values) is identified as an area for future work.

\section{Flood Risk Modeling}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Flood risk in the scope of this thesis refers to the expected monetary loss due to flooding, calculated by combining the hazard (flood depth), vulnerability (damage function), and exposure (asset value). This section presents the mathematical framework used to calculate total risk, distinguishing between losses for a specific return period and aggregated probabilistic estimates via \textbf{Expected Annual Damage (EAD)}.

\subsection{Risk for a Single Return Period}

For a given return period \( RP \), the expected loss at location \( (x, y) \in \Omega \) is given by:

\begin{equation}
	R(x, y; RP) = D(h(x, y, RP)) \cdot E(x, y)
	\label{eq:local-risk}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \( h(x, y, RP) \) is the flood depth at location \( (x, y) \) under return period \( RP \),
	\item \( D(h) \) is the depth--damage function,
	\item \( E(x, y) \) is the monetary value of the asset at \( (x, y) \).
\end{itemize}

This formulation corresponds to a scenario-based risk estimate, conditional on the occurrence of a specific flood event. For example, if a property at given location \( (x, y) \) is valued at €100,000, and the hazard model predicts \( h = 0.31\, \mathrm{m} \) under RP100, and \( D(0.31) = 0.17 \), the expected damage is:

\[
R(x, y; 100) = 0.17 \cdot 100{,}000\, \text{€} = 17{,}000\, \text{€}
\]

To estimate total loss over the study area \( \Omega \), the pointwise losses are integrated:

\begin{equation}
	R_{\text{total}}(RP) = \int_{\Omega} D(h(x, y, RP)) \cdot E(x, y) \, dx \, dy
	\label{eq:total-risk}
\end{equation}

For gridded data, this integral is approximated by a discrete sum:

\begin{equation}
	R_{\text{total}}(RP_k) \approx \sum_{i=1}^{N_x} \sum_{j=1}^{N_y} D(H_{i,j,k}) \cdot E_{i,j}
	\label{eq:total-risk-discrete}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Expected Annual Damage (EAD)}

While single-return-period losses are useful, financial and regulatory applications often require a risk indicator that incorporates the full probability distribution of flood events. The \textbf{Expected Annual Damage (EAD)} is such a measure: it aggregates flood risk across a range of return periods using their corresponding annual exceedance probabilities (AEPs).

Let \( RP_k \in \mathcal{RP} \) denote the set of modeled return periods, with associated exceedance probabilities \( AEP_k = 1/RP_k \). The EAD at location \( (x, y) \) is then:

\begin{equation}
	\text{EAD}(x, y) = \int_0^1 R(x, y; AEP) \, dAEP = \int_0^1 D(h(AEP)) \cdot E(x, y) \, dAEP
	\label{eq:ead-definition}
\end{equation}

In practice, this integral is approximated via trapezoidal integration over a discrete set of return periods:

\[
\text{EAD}(x, y) \approx \sum_{k=1}^{K-1} \frac{AEP_k - AEP_{k+1}}{2} \cdot \left[ R(x, y; RP_k) + R(x, y; RP_{k+1}) \right]
\]

This formulation captures both frequent and rare flood events, producing an annualized estimate of risk suitable for insurance, portfolio analysis, and regulatory reporting.

\subsection{Use Cases and Applications}

The risk modeling framework presented here supports several key applications:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Model comparison:} Evaluate differences in hazard predictions between global and local models.
	\item \textbf{Damage model validation:} Test how well global DDFs explain observed flood impacts in Slovenia.
	\item \textbf{EAD estimation:} Produce standardized annualized loss estimates for reporting and disclosure.
	\item \textbf{Sensitivity analysis:} Assess the impact of spatial resolution, proximity to water bodies, and hazard assumptions.
	\item \textbf{Modular integration:} Enable ingestion of local models into the Physrisk platform for institutional ESG workflows.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Preprocessing and Harmonization}

To ensure consistency with the hazard layers and vulnerability curves, the following steps are applied:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Geospatial normalization}: all coordinates are transformed to EPSG:4326; quality checks identify implausible points (e.g., outside country bounds).
    \item \textbf{Geospatial filtering}: only observations within the study area where both WRI Aqueduct and IKG models are defined are kept.
    \item \textbf{Depth QA}: reported depths are screened for unit consistency (meters), truncated to nonnegative values. Values larger than 4m are capped at 4m.
    \item \textbf{Damage QA}: reported damages where reported damage is larger than the property value are excluded from the dataset.
    \item \textbf{Damage normalization}: absolute damages (monetary losses, EUR) are converted, where possible, to \textit{relative damage} by normalizing to property value.
    \item \textbf{Property-type mapping}: property descriptors in the reports are mapped to the nearest JRC category to select the appropriate \(D(h)\) curve.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Intended Uses in This Thesis}

The curated dataset is used in two distinct ways:
\begin{description}
    \item[Hazard validation (Chapter~5.3):] For each observation, modelled depth at RP100 (from WRI and iKG100) is sampled at the reported coordinates and compared to the \emph{reported depth}. Metrics include regression error (MAE/RMSE), categorical accuracy across depth bands, and proximity bias analysis.
    \item[Vulnerability validation (Chapter~5.4):] For each observation, the DDF-predicted \emph{relative damage} \(D(h)\) (using the matched property-type curve and modelled or observed depths, as specified in the experiment) is compared to the \emph{observed relative damage}. Performance is summarized by error distributions and calibration plots across depth bins and property types.
\end{description}

Flood depth is the primary target variable for hazard model validation. It is analyzed both as:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A \textbf{continuous regression target} ranging from 0--4 m,
    \item A \textbf{categorical classification target} using three standardized classes:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Up to 0.5 m: 41\% of samples,
        \item 0.5–1.5 m: 42\% of samples,
        \item 1.5–2.5 m: 17\% of samples.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

The dataset reflects appraised damages and field-estimated depths and thus inherits several uncertainties: (i) measurement error in reported depths, (ii) heterogeneity in appraisal practices, (iii) potential location inaccuracies, and (iv) selection bias toward reported/insured events. No model is trained on this dataset; it is used exclusively for \emph{out-of-sample} validation of pre-existing hazard and vulnerability models.
