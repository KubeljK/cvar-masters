\chapter{Study Area, Data, and Methodology}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter introduces the data sources, mathematical models, and spatial context used to evaluate flood hazard and damage modeling in this thesis. The flood risk assessment is structured around three components: flood hazard (depth), vulnerability (damage functions), and exposure (property value). These components are defined both conceptually and mathematically, and are later integrated into a unified framework for risk estimation and validation.

%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Hazard and Depth}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Flood hazard refers to the spatial distribution and intensity of flooding under different return periods. In this study, hazard is represented by flood depth, modeled as a spatial function for given exceedance scenarios.

\subsection{Mathematical Definition of Flood Hazard}

Flood hazard is modeled as a spatially varying flood depth field:

\begin{equation}
	h : \Omega \times \mathcal{T} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}, \quad (x, y, T) \mapsto h(x, y, T)
	\label{eq:hazard-map}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \( \Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2 \) is the spatial domain of interest,
	\item \( \mathcal{T} = \{ T_1, T_2, \dots, T_K \} \) is the set of return periods considered (e.g., 10, 100, 500 years),
	\item \( h(x, y, T) \) is the flood depth (in meters) at location \( (x, y) \) for return period \( T \).
\end{itemize}

Return periods \( T \) express the average time between events of a given magnitude. The associated annual exceedance probability \( p \) is:

\begin{equation}
	p = \frac{1}{T}
	\label{eq:return-period}
\end{equation}

Flood hazard models estimate water depths \( h(x, y, T) \) using hydrological and hydraulic simulations that account for catchment runoff, river dynamics, topography, and in some cases, climate change. In practice, these functions are discretized into raster data aligned to a grid. The continuous function \( h(x, y, T) \) is approximated by an array \( H_{i,j,k} \), where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \( i, j \) index the spatial grid cells,
	\item \( k \) indexes the return period \( T_k \in \mathcal{T} \).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hazard Data Sources and Modeling Approach}

Two flood hazard models are evaluated and compared in this thesis:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{WRI Aqueduct River Flood Model:} This is a global flood hazard dataset developed by the World Resources Institute. It estimates flood depths using hydrological and hydraulic simulations from global river basins, based on historical river discharge, elevation models (e.g., MERIT DEM), and land surface parameters. The model provides depth estimates for return periods ranging from 2 to 1000 years, with global coverage at ~1 km resolution. Critically, it supports multiple future climate scenarios via Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs), specifically RCP4.5 and RCP8.5. These scenarios reflect moderate and high greenhouse gas emissions trajectories, respectively:
		\begin{itemize}
			\item \textbf{RCP4.5} assumes stabilization of radiative forcing at 4.5 W/m\textsuperscript{2} by 2100, representing moderate mitigation.
			\item \textbf{RCP8.5} assumes continued high emissions, leading to >4°C global warming by 2100.
		\end{itemize}
		Climate scenarios affect rainfall intensities, runoff, and river discharge, which in turn affect simulated flood extents and depths. The outputs are published in GeoTIFF raster format, using EPSG:4326 (WGS84).

	\item \textbf{Integralna karta globin (IKG):} This high-resolution local hazard dataset is developed by Slovenian civil protection authorities and technical consultants. It includes flood hazard maps for selected municipalities based on detailed hydraulic modeling (1D/2D simulations) calibrated using historical events. Flood extents are modeled under synthetic return period scenarios (10, 100, 500 years) and expressed in three discrete flood depth classes:
		\begin{itemize}
			\item GM – minor inundation (up to 0.5 m),
			\item GS – significant inundation (up to 1.5 m),
			\item GB – severe inundation (up to 2.5 m).
		\end{itemize}
		IKG data are originally stored as vector polygons (shapefiles or GeoPackages) using the national coordinate system (EPSG:3794). For integration and analysis, they are rasterized to a 5 m grid, harmonized with other layers, and stored in Zarr format. Unlike WRI, IKG models do not include climate scenario adjustments.
\end{itemize}

\section{Vulnerability and Damage Function}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Flood vulnerability quantifies the expected damage to an asset given the intensity of the flood at that location. This relationship is typically modeled via a depth--damage function.

\subsection{Mathematical Definition of Flood Vulnerability}

A depth--damage function \( D(h) \) maps flood depth \( h \) to the expected fractional loss in asset value:

\begin{equation}
	D : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \rightarrow [0, 1], \quad h \mapsto D(h)
	\label{eq:damage-function}
\end{equation}

The function \( D(h) \) is assumed to be monotonic and piecewise continuous. It starts at a nonzero depth threshold (typically 0.1–0.2 m), increases with water depth, and saturates at a maximum value (e.g., 0.7 to 0.9) depending on asset type.

	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/03/vulnerability_curve_europe.png}
	\caption[Depth--damage function for residential buildings.]{Standardized depth--damage function for residential buildings in Europe, as published by the Joint Research Centre (Huizinga et al., 2017). The curve shows how expected damage increases with flood depth \( h \), up to a saturation point near total loss.}
	\label{fig:ddf-jrc-europe}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Vulnerability Data Source}

This thesis uses standardized global depth--damage functions published by Huizinga et al.~\cite{huizinga_global_2017}, developed under the auspices of the European Commission Joint Research Centre (JRC). These functions are stratified by building type (e.g., residential, commercial, industrial) and were applied based on available metadata in the Slovenian dataset.

Damage reports from the Administration for Civil Protection and Disaster Relief of Slovenia serve as the primary reference for validation. These records include georeferenced flood locations, reported water depths, and economic losses. When available, building classifications were matched to appropriate JRC vulnerability curves.

\section{Flood Risk Modeling}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Flood risk refers to the expected financial loss due to flooding and is commonly expressed as a function of three interacting components: hazard, vulnerability, and exposure. This section presents a mathematical framework for calculating risk at the asset level and aggregating it across space and probability. It distinguishes between loss for a specific return period and Expected Annual Damage (EAD), which aggregates over all relevant scenarios.

\subsection{Risk for a Single Return Period}

For a given return period \( T \), the expected loss at a spatial location \( (x, y) \) is defined as:

\begin{equation}
	R(x, y; T) = D(h(x, y, T)) \cdot E(x, y)
	\label{eq:local-risk}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \( h(x, y, T) \) is the modeled flood depth (hazard),
	\item \( D(h) \) is the depth--damage function (vulnerability),
	\item \( E(x, y) \) is the value of the asset at location \( (x, y) \) (exposure).
\end{itemize}

This formulation reflects a scenario-based approach. For example, consider a property valued at €100,000. If the hazard model estimates a flood depth of \( h = 0.31 \, \mathrm{m} \) for the 100-year return period (\( T = 100 \)), and the damage function gives \( D(0.31) = 0.17 \), then the expected loss is:

\[
R(x, y; 100) = 0.17 \cdot 100{,}000 \, \text{€} = 17{,}000 \, \text{€}
\]

This estimate represents the expected damage conditional on a specific flood event.

To obtain total loss across a spatial region \( \Omega \), the losses are integrated (or summed for raster data):

\begin{equation}
	R_{\text{total}}(T) = \int_{\Omega} D(h(x, y, T)) \cdot E(x, y) \, dx \, dy
	\label{eq:total-risk}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
	R_{\text{total}}(T_k) \approx \sum_{i=1}^{N_x} \sum_{j=1}^{N_y} D(H_{i,j,k}) \cdot E_{i,j}
	\label{eq:total-risk-discrete}
\end{equation}

This discretized version is applied in this thesis to compare risk under different hazard models (WRI vs IKG) at specific return periods.

\subsection{Expected Annual Damage (EAD)}

While scenario-based losses (e.g., for RP100) are useful, they do not fully describe the stochastic nature of flood risk. A more comprehensive measure is the \textbf{Expected Annual Damage (EAD)}, which integrates risk across the full distribution of return periods or annual exceedance probabilities (AEPs).

Let \( p \in (0, 1] \) denote the AEP corresponding to return period \( T = 1/p \). Then the EAD at location \( (x, y) \) is given by:

\begin{equation}
	\text{EAD}(x, y) = \int_0^1 D(h(p)) \cdot E(x, y) \, dp
	\label{eq:ead-definition}
\end{equation}

In practice, this is approximated using trapezoidal integration over a discrete set of modeled return periods:

\[
\text{EAD}(x, y) \approx \sum_{k=1}^{K-1} \frac{p_k - p_{k+1}}{2} \cdot \left[ R(x, y; T_k) + R(x, y; T_{k+1}) \right]
\]

where \( \{T_k\} \) are the available return periods and \( p_k = 1/T_k \). This method captures both frequent and rare events in a single, annualized indicator.

\subsection{Exposure Data}

Exposure refers to the monetary value of physical assets potentially affected by flooding. For the Slovenian case study, the exposure values were derived from:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Appraised damage reports submitted to civil protection authorities,
	\item Official cadastral or tax-assessed values (when available),
	\item Synthetic estimates based on building typology and floor area.
\end{itemize}

All exposure estimates were spatially harmonized with hazard and vulnerability layers and aligned to a common grid. Values were normalized where necessary to match the assumptions of the damage function calibration (e.g., replacement cost basis).

\subsection{Use Cases}

This risk modeling framework supports multiple analytical and practical applications:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Model comparison:} Evaluate spatial accuracy of WRI vs IKG hazard inputs.
	\item \textbf{Vulnerability validation:} Assess the applicability of global depth--damage functions using local empirical data.
	\item \textbf{EAD calculation:} Derive annualized loss estimates for integration into financial and regulatory workflows.
	\item \textbf{Sensitivity analysis:} Examine the influence of spatial resolution and waterbody proximity on model performance.
	\item \textbf{Platform integration:} Enable modular onboarding into the Physrisk API and ESG disclosure platforms.
\end{itemize}
