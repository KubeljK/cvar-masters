\chapter{Study Area, Data, and Methodology}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This chapter defines the geographic and empirical scope of the study and outlines the data sources and methodological approaches used to evaluate flood hazard and vulnerability models. The objective is to establish a clear foundation for the experimental work presented in later chapters by describing the spatial context, data quality, and analytical framework adopted in this thesis.

First, the chapter introduces the two primary flood hazard models under investigation: the global WRI Aqueduct River Flood model and the local Slovenian Integralna karta globin (IKG) model. These models are compared in terms of resolution, coverage, return period granularity, and underlying assumptions.

Second, the chapter presents the vulnerability component of the flood risk framework, focusing on standardized depth--damage functions developed by the European Commission Joint Research Centre (JRC). These functions are used to estimate expected economic damage from flooding based on inundation depth and building type.

Lastly, the chapter describes the overall flood risk modeling approach, structured according to the widely adopted hazard--vulnerability--exposure paradigm. This includes the assumptions used for data transformation, the structure of the validation dataset, and the rationale behind the chosen evaluation metrics.

The chapter is structured as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Section~3.1 describes the hazard models and their flood depth data characteristics.
    \item Section~3.2 presents the vulnerability modeling approach using depth--damage functions.
    \item Section~3.3 integrates hazard, vulnerability, and exposure into a unified flood risk modeling framework.
\end{itemize}

%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Hazard and Depth}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Flood hazard refers to the likelihood and severity of flooding at a specific location over a defined time horizon. In this thesis, hazard is represented by flood depth estimates associated with given return periods (e.g., 10-, 100-, or 500-year floods). These estimates form the input to downstream risk calculations and serve as the basis for comparing model performance.

This section describes the two flood hazard models used in the study: the global WRI Aqueduct River Flood model and the local Slovenian Integralna karta globin (IKG) model. The models differ significantly in spatial resolution, geographic coverage, underlying hydrological assumptions, and treatment of climate scenarios. Their characteristics are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:hazard_models}.

\subsection{WRI Aqueduct River Flood Model}

The \textit{World Resources Institute (WRI) Aqueduct River Flood} model is a publicly available global flood hazard dataset that provides inundation depth estimates at a spatial resolution of approximately 1 km. It is designed for large-scale screening of riverine flood risks and supports a wide range of return periods, typically including RP2, RP5, RP10, RP25, RP50, RP100, RP250, RP500, and RP1000.

The model incorporates climate scenario data by linking river discharge estimates to Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs), particularly RCP4.5 and RCP8.5. It uses a combination of global hydrological models and digital elevation data to simulate flood extents and depths under historical and future climate conditions.

In this thesis, the WRI model is used as a benchmark for evaluating local model performance under the 100-year return period (RP100). While it lacks the spatial granularity needed for parcel-level risk assessment, it offers consistent global coverage and climate-adjusted projections.

\subsection{Integralna karta globin (IKG)}

The \textit{Integralna karta globin (IKG)} is a collection of high-resolution flood hazard models developed for selected municipalities in Slovenia. Each model is based on detailed hydrological and hydraulic simulations conducted by local engineering firms, using fine-scale terrain data and historical flood records. The original data is stored in vector formats (e.g., shapefiles, GeoPackages), with flood depth zones categorized into three discrete classes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item GM: Minor inundation (0.5 m depth)
    \item GS: Significant inundation (1.5 m depth)
    \item GB: Severe inundation (2.5 m depth)
\end{itemize}

IKG models are available for return periods of 10, 100, and 500 years (RP10, RP100, RP500) and are typically generated in EPSG:3794 projection. For this thesis, the IKG layers from 18 areas of importance (\textit{Območja pomembnega vpliva}) were harmonized, rasterized into a 5×5 m grid, and integrated into a unified hazard layer (as described in Chapter~4).

Although IKG models are not climate-scenario-aware, they are more spatially detailed than global models and are considered to better represent flood dynamics at the neighborhood or parcel level.

\subsection{Return Periods and Depth Representation}

Both hazard models use a return-period-based approach to represent flood hazard. A return period \( T \) indicates the average time between flood events of a given magnitude and is used to estimate the annual exceedance probability \( p = 1/T \).

Flood depths are modeled either as continuous variables (WRI) or discrete depth classes (IKG). For consistent comparison, the IKG model was converted into a raster with fixed depth values for each class. For WRI, continuous flood depths are directly extracted for the RP100 layer and compared against observed values.

\subsection{Hazard Data Extraction}

For model comparison and validation, flood depth predictions from both WRI and IKG were extracted for each point in the validation dataset corresponding to flood events in Slovenia between 2010 and 2023. All extractions were performed for the 100-year return period (RP100) using the toolset described in Section~4.4. The extracted depths were then compared to observed values using regression and classification metrics, as discussed in Chapter~5.

This dual-model approach enables an empirical assessment of the trade-offs between resolution and scalability and provides insight into the suitability of global models for local decision-making.

\section{Vulnerability and Damage Function}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Vulnerability in the context of flood risk refers to the degree of damage an asset is expected to incur when exposed to a flood of a given intensity. In quantitative flood risk models, vulnerability is typically represented by a \textit{depth--damage function} that expresses the expected percentage of asset damage as a function of inundation depth.

This section describes the vulnerability modeling approach adopted in this thesis. It focuses on the use of standardized global depth--damage functions, their assumptions, and how they are applied to the Slovenian flood impact dataset. The function used here originates from the Joint Research Centre (JRC) of the European Commission and is part of a global initiative to provide harmonized vulnerability data for risk assessment.

\subsection{Depth--Damage Functions}

A depth--damage function (DDF) maps flood depth \( h \) (in meters) to expected relative damage \( D(h) \), typically expressed as a fraction or percentage of the total asset value. These functions are generally derived from empirical post-disaster surveys, insurance claims data, or synthetic modelling. They vary by asset type, construction material, country, and socio-economic context.

In this study, we use the depth--damage functions published by Huizinga et al.~\cite{huizinga_global_2017}, which are among the most widely used sources for flood damage estimation at the European level. These functions are defined for different building types (e.g., residential, commercial, industrial) and are based on a normalized building replacement value (BRV).

\subsection{Function Structure and Properties}

The JRC functions have the following properties:
\begin{itemize}
    \item They are monotonic and piecewise continuous,
    \item Damage begins at a threshold of approximately 0.1–0.2 m and increases with depth,
    \item Maximum damage is capped between 70\% and 90\% depending on asset type,
    \item Uncertainty is not explicitly modeled, but curves represent expected mean damage.
\end{itemize}

An example residential depth--damage function is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ddf_example}. In this model, a 0.5 m flood may cause damage equivalent to 15–25\% of the building's replacement value, while a 2.5 m flood could exceed 70\%.

\subsection{Application to Slovenian Flood Events}

To evaluate the applicability of global depth--damage functions in a local context, we apply the JRC DDFs to a dataset of reported flood damages in Slovenia. This dataset, obtained from the Administration for Civil Protection and Disaster Relief, contains:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Georeferenced flood locations,
    \item Reported flood depths (rounded or estimated by appraisal),
    \item Reported economic damage (in EUR),
    \item Property classification (e.g., residential, commercial, public use).
\end{itemize}

The reported damages are normalized by the building replacement value (when available or estimated), resulting in a relative damage metric. Predicted damage from the DDF is then compared to observed relative damage to assess model performance. The comparison is segmented by property type and flood depth category.

\subsection{Assumptions and Limitations}

While the JRC depth--damage functions offer a standardized approach, several assumptions and limitations must be noted:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Transferability:} The functions are designed for generalized application and may not reflect local building codes, materials, or insurance systems in Slovenia.
    \item \textbf{No uncertainty modeling:} The functions provide point estimates of expected damage, not distributions. However, empirical data show that actual damages are often bimodal or skewed.
    \item \textbf{Property segmentation:} Limited metadata in the Slovenian dataset constrains the ability to differentiate vulnerability by building age, elevation, or structural resilience.
\end{itemize}

Despite these limitations, the DDFs serve as a useful benchmark for evaluating vulnerability model performance. Further improvements are discussed in Section~5.4 and Chapter~6.

\subsection{Link to Risk Estimation}

The vulnerability model is used in conjunction with flood depth predictions from hazard models to compute expected damage at each location:
\[
\text{Expected Damage} = D(h) \times \text{Asset Value}
\]
This formulation assumes deterministic vulnerability, where the same flood depth always results in the same relative damage. This simplification facilitates large-scale analysis but may under-represent epistemic and aleatory uncertainty. These limitations are acknowledged in the discussion.

In the next section, we present the overall structure of the flood risk model used in this study and describe how hazard, vulnerability, and exposure components are integrated.


\section{Flood Risk Modeling}

Flood risk is commonly conceptualized as the intersection of three key components: hazard, vulnerability, and exposure. This approach is consistent with methodologies used in climate risk disclosure frameworks such as the Network for Greening the Financial System (NGFS), the EU Taxonomy, and the OS-Climate Physrisk platform. In this section, we formalize how these components are operationalized in this thesis to estimate physical flood risk at the property level.

\subsection{Risk Definition}

Following the standard formulation, flood risk \( R \) is defined as:
\[
R = H \times V \times E
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \( H \) is the hazard, represented by the probability and intensity (depth) of flooding for a given return period,
    \item \( V \) is the vulnerability, expressed as a depth--damage function mapping flood depth to expected relative damage,
    \item \( E \) is the exposure, typically the asset value (e.g., building replacement value) at the location in question.
\end{itemize}

In this formulation, risk can be expressed in monetary terms as the expected annual damage (EAD) or scenario-specific loss (e.g., loss under a 100-year flood). This thesis focuses on evaluating model performance for the RP100 return period.

\subsection{Hazard Component}

As described in Section~3.1, the hazard component is derived from either the global WRI Aqueduct River Flood model or the local IKG model. For each validation point, flood depth predictions are extracted at RP100 and used as input to the vulnerability function.

In areas where both models are available, their predictions are compared side-by-side using observed flood depths from past events as a reference. The evaluation metrics include regression error, classification accuracy across depth bands, and distance from reported measurements.

\subsection{Vulnerability Component}

As detailed in Section~3.2, the vulnerability component uses standardized depth--damage functions developed by the JRC. These functions convert predicted flood depths into expected relative damage fractions. The predicted damage values are then compared against reported economic losses in Slovenia, normalized by estimated asset values.

\subsection{Exposure Component}

The exposure term \( E \) is represented by the estimated or reported value of the affected asset. In the validation dataset, appraised building values are used when available; otherwise, standardized assumptions based on national averages are applied. While this introduces potential error, the relative damage metric allows for comparison independent of absolute valuation accuracy.

\subsection{Implementation Workflow}

The computational steps for flood risk estimation are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item For each point in the validation dataset:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Extract flood depth from WRI and IKG models for RP100,
        \item Apply the JRC depth--damage function to compute expected relative damage,
        \item Multiply by estimated asset value to obtain monetary loss estimate (if applicable).
    \end{itemize}
    \item Compare model predictions (depth and damage) with observed values from reported flood events.
    \item Segment results by model type, proximity to water bodies, and property classification.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Use Cases and Relevance}

The flood risk modeling framework developed in this thesis supports a range of applications, including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Empirical evaluation of model accuracy for flood depth and damage estimation,
    \item Validation of global vulnerability functions in local contexts,
    \item Comparative analysis between coarse-resolution and high-resolution hazard models,
    \item Integration into ESG-aligned climate risk disclosure pipelines.
\end{itemize}

This model architecture is aligned with the structure of the Physrisk platform and is used in subsequent chapters to evaluate the performance of hazard and vulnerability models on observed flood events in Slovenia.
