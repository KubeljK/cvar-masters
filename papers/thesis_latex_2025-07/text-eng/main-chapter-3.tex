\chapter{Study Area, Data, and Methodology}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter introduces the geographic scope, data sources, and mathematical models used to evaluate flood hazard, vulnerability, and risk. The modeling framework is structured around three key components: \textbf{hazard} (depth), \textbf{vulnerability} (damage functions), and \textbf{exposure} (asset value). These elements are formally defined and then combined to compute expected flood losses under both scenario-based and probabilistic approaches.

%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Hazard and Depth}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Flood hazard refers to the intensity and spatial extent of flooding, typically expressed as inundation depth for different return periods. In this study, hazard is represented as a function that maps location and return period to predicted water depth.

\subsection{Mathematical Definition of Flood Hazard}

Flood hazard is modeled as a spatially varying flood depth field:

\begin{equation}
	h : \Omega \times \mathcal{T} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}, \quad (x, y, T) \mapsto h(x, y, T)
	\label{eq:hazard-map}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \( \Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2 \) is the spatial domain (study area),
	\item \( \mathcal{T} = \{ T_1, T_2, \dots, T_K \} \) is the set of modeled return periods (e.g., 10, 100, 500 years),
	\item \( h(x, y, T) \) is the flood depth at location \( (x, y) \) under return period \( T \).
\end{itemize}

The return period \( T \) expresses the average time between flood events of that magnitude, while the annual exceedance probability (AEP) is its inverse:

\begin{equation}
	p = \frac{1}{T}
	\label{eq:return-period}
\end{equation}

Flood hazard maps \( h(x, y, T) \) are derived from hydrological and hydraulic models that simulate flood dynamics based on rainfall, runoff, topography, and river systems. In practice, the continuous function \( h(x, y, T) \) is approximated by raster arrays \( H_{i,j,k} \), where \( i,j \) index the spatial grid and \( k \) indexes the return period.

\subsection{Hazard Data Sources and Modeling Approach}

Two complementary hazard datasets are used in this study:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{WRI Aqueduct River Flood Model:} A global flood hazard dataset developed by the World Resources Institute. It provides flood depth estimates at ~1 km resolution for return periods from 2 to 1000 years, using simulations based on historical river discharge, terrain data (e.g., MERIT DEM), and land surface parameters. Critically, this model incorporates climate scenarios through Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs), including:
		\begin{itemize}
			\item \textbf{RCP4.5}: A moderate emissions pathway, stabilizing radiative forcing at 4.5 W/m\textsuperscript{2} by 2100.
			\item \textbf{RCP8.5}: A high-emissions scenario, corresponding to >4\textdegree C warming by 2100.
		\end{itemize}
		Outputs are provided in GeoTIFF raster format (EPSG:4326).

	\item \textbf{Integralna karta globin (IKG):} A set of high-resolution flood hazard maps produced by Slovenian civil protection authorities. These are based on 1D/2D hydraulic modeling for return periods of 10, 100, and 500 years. The original data are provided as vector polygons categorized into three flood depth bands:
		\begin{itemize}
			\item GM – Minor inundation (up to 0.5 m),
			\item GS – Significant inundation (up to 1.5 m),
			\item GB – Severe inundation (up to 2.5 m).
		\end{itemize}
		IKG models are locally calibrated using historical flood events and topographic surveys. Vector inputs are rasterized to a 5 m grid in EPSG:3794 and harmonized with the other spatial layers.
\end{itemize}

To enable direct comparison and model evaluation, all flood depth maps are resampled to a common 5 m grid and spatial reference system. These harmonized hazard layers serve as inputs to the vulnerability and risk modeling framework described in the following sections.

\section{Vulnerability and Damage Function}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Flood vulnerability quantifies the degree to which an asset is susceptible to damage when exposed to flooding. In quantitative modeling, this is typically represented using \textbf{depth--damage functions} (DDFs), which estimate the fraction of asset value lost as a function of water depth.

\subsection{Mathematical Definition of Flood Vulnerability}

A depth--damage function \( D(h) \) maps flood depth \( h \) (in meters) to the expected relative damage \( D(h) \in [0, 1] \):

\begin{equation}
	D : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \rightarrow [0, 1], \quad h \mapsto D(h)
	\label{eq:damage-function}
\end{equation}

These functions are typically monotonically increasing and piecewise continuous. They begin at a damage threshold (e.g., 0.1--0.2 m) and increase until they saturate near the total loss level (typically 70--90\% of asset value), depending on construction type and use.

\begin{figure}[htb]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/03/vulnerability_curve_europe.png}
	\caption[Depth--damage function for residential buildings.]{Standardized depth--damage function for residential buildings in Europe, based on Huizinga et al.~\cite{huizinga_global_2017}. The curve indicates increasing expected damage with flood depth, saturating near total loss.}
	\label{fig:ddf-jrc-europe}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Vulnerability Data Source}

This thesis uses a set of standardized depth--damage functions developed by the European Commission Joint Research Centre (JRC) and published by Huizinga et al.~\cite{huizinga_global_2017}. These functions are among the most widely used tools for flood damage estimation in Europe.

The DDFs are stratified by:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Asset type} (e.g., residential, commercial, industrial),
	\item \textbf{Damage component} (structure, contents, or total),
	\item \textbf{Construction context} (regional economic and building typologies).
\end{itemize}

For each property in the Slovenian flood dataset, a vulnerability curve was selected based on asset classification. All damage functions are calibrated relative to building replacement value (BRV), which serves as the basis for expressing relative damage.

\subsection{Validation Dataset}

Observed flood damage data were obtained from the Administration for Civil Protection and Disaster Relief of Slovenia. This dataset includes:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Georeferenced flood-affected locations,
	\item Estimated or recorded flood depths,
	\item Reported monetary damage in EUR,
	\item Metadata on property type and use (where available).
\end{itemize}

Reported values were normalized by estimated replacement value, allowing comparison with DDF predictions. Where metadata was missing, building type was inferred using cadastral layers or estimated from available zoning attributes.

\subsection{Assumptions and Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Transferability:} The JRC functions are designed for generalized European use and may not perfectly reflect local construction practices in Slovenia.
	\item \textbf{Uncertainty:} The functions represent expected values and do not account for uncertainty or variance in actual damage.
	\item \textbf{Data completeness:} Some events in the validation dataset lack full metadata or have coarse depth estimates, limiting precision.
\end{itemize}

Despite these limitations, the global vulnerability curves offer a consistent and replicable benchmark for testing flood risk models and have been widely used in EU-level assessments.

\section{Flood Risk Modeling}
%--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Flood risk refers to the expected monetary loss due to flooding, calculated by combining the hazard (flood depth), vulnerability (damage function), and exposure (asset value). This section presents the mathematical framework used in this thesis, distinguishing between losses for a specific return period and aggregated probabilistic estimates via \textbf{Expected Annual Damage (EAD)}.

\subsection{Risk for a Single Return Period}

For a given return period \( T \), the expected loss at location \( (x, y) \in \Omega \) is given by:

\begin{equation}
	R(x, y; T) = D(h(x, y, T)) \cdot E(x, y)
	\label{eq:local-risk}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \( h(x, y, T) \) is the flood depth at location \( (x, y) \) under return period \( T \),
	\item \( D(h) \) is the depth--damage function,
	\item \( E(x, y) \) is the monetary value of the asset at \( (x, y) \).
\end{itemize}

This formulation corresponds to a scenario-based risk estimate, conditional on the occurrence of a specific flood event. For example, if a building is worth €100,000, and the hazard model predicts \( h = 0.31\, \mathrm{m} \) under RP100, and \( D(0.31) = 0.17 \), the expected damage is:

\[
R(x, y; 100) = 0.17 \cdot 100{,}000\, \text{€} = 17{,}000\, \text{€}
\]

To estimate total loss over the study area \( \Omega \), the pointwise losses are integrated:

\begin{equation}
	R_{\text{total}}(T) = \int_{\Omega} D(h(x, y, T)) \cdot E(x, y) \, dx \, dy
	\label{eq:total-risk}
\end{equation}

For gridded data, this integral is approximated by a discrete sum:

\begin{equation}
	R_{\text{total}}(T_k) \approx \sum_{i=1}^{N_x} \sum_{j=1}^{N_y} D(H_{i,j,k}) \cdot E_{i,j}
	\label{eq:total-risk-discrete}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Expected Annual Damage (EAD)}

While single-return-period losses are useful, financial and regulatory applications often require a risk indicator that incorporates the full probability distribution of flood events. The \textbf{Expected Annual Damage (EAD)} is such a measure: it aggregates flood risk across a range of return periods using their corresponding annual exceedance probabilities (AEPs).

Let \( T_k \in \mathcal{T} \) denote the set of modeled return periods, with associated exceedance probabilities \( p_k = 1/T_k \). The EAD at location \( (x, y) \) is then:

\begin{equation}
	\text{EAD}(x, y) = \int_0^1 R(x, y; p) \, dp = \int_0^1 D(h(p)) \cdot E(x, y) \, dp
	\label{eq:ead-definition}
\end{equation}

In practice, this integral is approximated via trapezoidal integration over a discrete set of return periods:

\[
\text{EAD}(x, y) \approx \sum_{k=1}^{K-1} \frac{p_k - p_{k+1}}{2} \cdot \left[ R(x, y; T_k) + R(x, y; T_{k+1}) \right]
\]

This formulation captures both frequent and rare flood events, producing an annualized estimate of risk suitable for insurance, portfolio analysis, and regulatory reporting.

\subsection{Exposure Data}

Exposure refers to the monetary value of physical assets affected by flooding. In this thesis, exposure is represented in a simplified form using fixed nominal values. This was done to focus the analysis on hazard and vulnerability model performance, rather than on variability in asset valuation.

In the illustrative examples and validation scenarios, a synthetic property value of €100,000 was used. This allowed flood risk to be modeled consistently across different hazard and vulnerability combinations.

Importantly, the validation analysis was conducted in \textbf{relative terms} — comparing observed and predicted \textit{fractional damage} (i.e., damage as a share of estimated property value), rather than in absolute monetary losses. This avoids the need for detailed cadastral or tax registry data and reduces dependence on asset-specific exposure assumptions.

While this approach abstracts away exposure uncertainty, it enables transparent model comparison. Integration of more granular exposure data (e.g. official appraisal values) is identified as an area for future work.



\subsection{Use Cases and Applications}

The risk modeling framework presented here supports several key applications:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Model comparison:} Evaluate differences in hazard predictions between global and local models.
	\item \textbf{Damage model validation:} Test how well global DDFs explain observed flood impacts in Slovenia.
	\item \textbf{EAD estimation:} Produce standardized annualized loss estimates for reporting and disclosure.
	\item \textbf{Sensitivity analysis:} Assess the impact of spatial resolution, proximity to water bodies, and hazard assumptions.
	\item \textbf{Modular integration:} Enable ingestion of local models into the Physrisk platform for institutional ESG workflows.
\end{itemize}
