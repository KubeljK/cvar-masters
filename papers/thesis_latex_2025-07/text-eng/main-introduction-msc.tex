\section{Background and Motivation}

Flooding is one of the most frequent and damaging natural hazards globally, responsible for extensive economic losses, social disruption, and loss of life. Climate change is expected to further intensify these risks by increasing both the frequency and severity of hydrological extremes. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) projects that intense rainfall and flood-related disasters will become more frequent, especially in areas experiencing greater variability in water systems and rising sea levels \cite{ipcc2021}.

In Europe, recent catastrophic flood events — such as those in Germany and Belgium in 2021, and in Slovenia in 2023 — have exposed significant vulnerabilities in infrastructure, spatial planning, and emergency preparedness. According to the European Environment Agency (EEA), riverine and pluvial floods already account for more than one-third of total weather-related losses in Europe, a share that is projected to increase over coming decades without improved mitigation and adaptation measures \cite{eea2020floods}.

These developments underscore the urgent need for robust and spatially detailed flood risk models that can support decision-making in domains such as insurance pricing, urban planning, asset valuation, and regulatory compliance. This is particularly critical for stakeholders in the real estate and financial sectors, who are increasingly required to assess and disclose exposure to climate-related physical risks as part of their environmental, social, and governance (ESG) reporting obligations \cite{ecb2020, tcfd2017}.


\section{Climate Risk and ESG Disclosure}

Climate risk refers to the potential harmful effects of climate change on economic systems, institutions, and physical assets. It is commonly classified into three interrelated categories: \textit{physical}, \textit{transition}, and \textit{liability} risks \cite{moorhouse2024}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Physical risks} arise from the direct consequences of climate variability and long-term climate change. These include \textit{acute} hazards such as floods, storms, and wildfires, as well as \textit{chronic} hazards such as sea-level rise and shifting temperature trends. These risks can cause immediate physical damage and long-term degradation of asset value.
    
    \item \textbf{Transition risks} arise from the transition to a low-carbon economy. They include policy changes (e.g., carbon pricing, emissions regulations), changes in technology, shifting market preferences, and the reputational risk of falling behind or not keeping up with the latest developments.
    
    \item \textbf{Liability risks} are also included by some frameworks \cite{woetzel2020climate}, referring to legal claims for compensation by those affected by anthropogenic climate change.
\end{itemize}

These categories are increasingly embedded in ESG-aligned regulatory frameworks such as those developed by the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) and the Network for Greening the Financial System (NGFS). In the European context, the European Central Bank (ECB) requires significant institutions to assess, manage, and disclose climate-related and environmental risks, including physical risks. According to the ECB’s supervisory guidance, banks are expected to integrate these risks into their internal governance, risk management, stress testing, and disclosure practices \cite{ecb2020}.

Physical risk is typically modeled as the interaction of three fundamental components:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hazard} - the probability and intensity of a climate-related event (e.g., flood depth for a 100-year return period),
    \item \textbf{Vulnerability} - the expected damage to an asset given the intensity of the hazard,
    \item \textbf{Exposure} - the presence and value of assets located in potentially affected areas.
\end{itemize}

These components form the foundation of physical climate risk models. However, despite the wide adoption of this conceptual framework, practical implementation often suffers from limited model transparency, inconsistent spatial resolution, and poor validation — especially at the local scale relevant to property-level investment and risk management.

This thesis focuses specifically on \textbf{acute physical risks}, with a particular emphasis on \textbf{riverine flooding} and its financial impact on real estate assets. By comparing the performance of global and local flood models and evaluating standardized vulnerability functions against observed data, this paper aims to contribute to more accurate and transparent risk quantification in support of ESG-aligned decision-making.


\section{Problem Statement}

While global flood hazard models such as the \textit{WRI Aqueduct River Floods} provide broad coverage and climate scenario integration, they are often limited in spatial resolution and may not reflect local hydrological conditions. On the other hand, high-resolution local models, such as Slovenia’s \textit{Integralna karta globin} (IKG), offer detailed inundation mapping but are typically fragmented, static (i.e., not climate scenario-aware), and difficult to integrate into broader risk platforms.

Furthermore, flood vulnerability is typically modeled using generalized depth-damage functions derived from empirical or synthetic data. These functions may perform well in aggregate, but their applicability across different building types, geographies, and damage-reporting standards remains uncertain. This creates uncertainty in downstream financial estimates and limits the usefulness of such models for asset- or portfolio-level risk assessment.

\section{Research Objectives}

The purpose of this thesis is to evaluate how well existing flood models can represent real-world flood depths and damage outcomes in Slovenia. Specifically, it compares a global hazard model (WRI Aqueduct) and a high-resolution local model (IKG) to assess the accuracy of their flood depth predictions.


Separately, the thesis tests global depth-damage functions—used to estimate flood-related property value loss—against reported damage data. The goal is to evaluate their accuracy and explore ways to improve them. Taken together, these analyses assess how reliable current modeling tools are for understanding physical climate risk and for supporting property-related decisions in the face of growing flood exposure.

To support this, the thesis builds on the \textit{Physrisk} platform—an open-source framework for integrating hazard, vulnerability, and exposure models into a unified physical climate risk assessment pipeline.

The main goals of the research are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item {To improve existing physrisk risk assessment framework with the following contributions:}
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item {Develop a data processing pipeline for creating a unified and homogenous SI IKG flood depth model for different areas of importance.}
        \item {Develop a data onboarding pipeline for integrating this new unified model into physrisk framework.}
    \end{enumerate}
    \item {To compare the performance of a global flood hazard model (WRI Aqueduct) and a local Slovenian model (IKG) using real flood data.}
    \item {To evaluate how model resolution and proximity to water bodies affect prediction accuracy.}
    \item {To assess the accuracy of global flood depth–damage functions on reported damage cases in Slovenia and improve their performance.}

\end{enumerate}

\section{Methodological Approach}

This thesis adopts a comparative modeling approach using geospatial and statistical techniques. A validation dataset—compiled from the records of the Slovenian Administration for Civil Protection and Disaster Relief (\textit{Uprava za zaščito in reševanje})—includes appraised damage values and measured flood depths for selected events between 2010 and 2023.

Flood hazard models are evaluated using both regression-based and classification-based metrics to assess depth prediction accuracy. Vulnerability models are tested by comparing observed versus predicted damages using segmented analysis by flood depth and real estate type.

From a scientific perspective, this work contributes to the empirical validation of global flood hazard and vulnerability models in a local Central European context. From a practical standpoint, it improves the existing flood risk assessment framework, allowing for easier model intercomparison and usage of existing models in new contexts.


\section{Structure of the Thesis}

The remainder of the thesis is structured as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Chapter 2} presents a review of relevant literature and related work on flood hazard modeling, vulnerability estimation, and ESG-aligned risk frameworks.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 3} describes the study area, datasets, and the methodological framework used for model evaluation and data processing.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 4} details the integration of the local IKG model into the Physrisk framework and presents the architecture of the comparison toolset.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 5} provides experimental results, including quantitative evaluation of model performance, depth-damage function analysis, and financial impact estimates.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 6} discusses the key findings, limitations, and possible future research directions, and concludes the thesis.
\end{itemize}
